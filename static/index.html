A Cryptocurrency

Bitcoin is the world's first decentralized digital currency. This means Bitcoin works without any kind of central bank or administrative system.
In 2008, an unknown person or group published a paper describing a digital currency under the name of Satoshi Nakamoto. One year later, a reference implementation was made available as an open source-project by them.
Bitcoin works by using a public distributed ledger (called blockchain) that allows clients to place transactions to be recorded by the network. It takes approximately 10 minutes to pass a transaction using the current system.
Each person that wants to trade Bitcoins uses a client that connects to the Bitcoin network. The client manages the distributed ledger and the communication with the rest of the network.

Mining

Because the ledger is public, everybody can pass transactions using it. We then need some way to validate that a transaction is authentic, otherwise people could steal each other's money by lying on the ledger. This is made possible thanks to cryptography and the concept of signatures.
But somebody might still be able to lie to somebody else. If I owe you money, I could tell your client that I actually gave it to you but keep this information from the rest of the network. That way, everyone but you will believe I still have the same amount of money as before. Hence the idea of the Bitcoin challenge.
Basically, someone wanting to pass a transaction using the blockchain (our public ledger) needs to do some kind of computation that is actually very costly timewise. They can then add a block to the blockchain that validates a bunch of transactions. A direct consequence of this is that the bigger the computing power used for this task, the harder it is for a single actor to beat them at fullfilling it, simply because a single group or person can hardly possess enough computing power to beat the rest of the world.
One more thing, we can make every block in the blockchain linked to the block before it so the state of the next block is dependent on the state of the whole chain. That way, nobody can modify previous blocks without invalidating the rest of the chain. We are now tamper proof!
However, we still have a problem. People are not going to spend a bunch of time and energy to pass transactions for free, so we need to reward them. That's where the term "mining" comes from. Everytime somebody validates the challenge and appends a block to the chain, they can add a transaction to give themselves Bitcoins out of nowhere! This way they are properly rewarded for spending all of these resources.

Forks

Because Bitcoin is essentially software, people are going to want to modify it in some way. However, it is unlikely that everybody is on board with every change so sometimes, we get a fork.
A fork is basically a divergence in the blockchain due to some changes to the rules of the challenge used to reach consensus. There are two types of forks, hard forks and soft forks.
Hard forks happen when the new clients (using the new rules) in the network reject the old ones (or vice-versa). So we essentially have a new currency that shares history with the first one. If everybody upgrades, then we're fine but if people disagree we might get some problems. We don't want people to be able to spend their past money twice after all.
Soft forks are kind of like hard forks, except that we do not split the community in two parts hoping that either one changes their mind. We introduce new rules, and try to get a majority of the network to adopt them but still keep a way to be compatible with the protocol before the changes. That way we can easily introduce a new concept in the protocol without breaking everything.

An Update

The 15th of July 2017, the value of Bitcoin dropped. One of the main reasons is the first of August getting closer. At that time, Bitcoin was about to get an update. Miners would be able to fit more transactions inside of a single block which would bring the average time needed to validate transactions down. This change was backwards-compatible and can thus be called a soft fork. Nevertheless, uncertainty related to these changes had a negative impact on the currency.

Bitcoin Cash

Following a lot of discussions about Bitcoin and its relative slugishness when compared to centralized banking systems, the community decided to try and make the system faster. The update we talked about in the previous paragraph sought to do this by changin the information inside of a block but another idea came about: make blocks bigger. Bitcoin Cash was born, a hard fork that allowed it's user to mine blocks about 8 times bigger than Bitcoin, which means 8 times as many transactions could be validated using a single block. The fork was deemed a failure by some parts of the community due to its poor initial adoption. In the weeks following its creation, Bitcoin Cash was nevertheless fighting toe to toe with Bitcoin in terms of profitability.

Bitcoin Gold

Le fork Bitcoin Gold sort le 23 octobre 2017, son objectif est d'empêcher la domination de machines de minage spécialisées (ASIC). Les acheteurs de Bitcoin deviennent frileux, donc le cours du Bitcoin diminue. Cependant rien de concret ne se passe, comme en témoigne l'historique du nombre de calculs effectués par les mineurs. Cela est dû au fait qu'aucun portefeuille, client ou mineur n'est encore disponible.

SegWit2x

Le 8 novembre 2017, après l'annonce de la création du fork SegWit2x, son déploiement son déploiement est annulé. Cela a pour effet de rassurer les utilisateurs. Le Bitcoin atteint ce jour-là une valeur record.

Bitcoin Gold - La Revanche

Le week-end du 12 novembre 2017 est marqué par la sortie du client pour le fork Bitcoin Gold. Avant cela, Bitcoin Gold n'avait qu'une valeur théorique, sans portefeuille, client ou mineur. L'arrivée d'un concurrent potentiel effraie les utilisateurs, qui n'osent plus acheter de Bitcoin, et en fait chuter la valeur en USD. Il est intéressant de constater que les calculs effectués par les mineurs ont diminué de 50% durant le week-end. La raison semble évidente : les mineurs ne voulant pas être en reste si Bitcoin Gold s'avérait populaire, ont utilisé leurs machines pour miner du Bitcoin Gold dès que possible.
